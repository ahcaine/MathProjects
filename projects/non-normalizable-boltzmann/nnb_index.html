Modelling With Randomness

This page is under construction! This project is a rebuild from an older school project for Math 522: Random Walks, at Colorado State University. The original MATLAB code was lost when I graduated, but I fed AI my old write up and had it convert the project to python but I have yet to go through and validate it. My idea for this project:

  • Introduce Brownian Motion as a natural phenomena.
  • Introduce modeling Brownian Motion via iterative randomly generated steps.
  • Demonstrate how averages are used to study randomness. In practical terms this often means running many simulations and averaging the trials to get a feel for how the system tends to evolve. (Averaging trials of simple brownian motion results in guassian diffusion.)
  • Introduce my old project and the paper(s) it references and demonstrate how stochastic processes are used in Statistical Physics. (Trajectories are probabilistic but we can still talk about where an average trajectory tends to travel.)
    • Technical -> My particular project used an overdamped Langevin particle near a charged wall, compares the constant-diffusivity dynamics to a long-time scaling profile, and then studies position-dependent diffusivity models that produce near-linear MSD with non-Gaussian displacement statistics.
    • Non-technical -> Electrostatic forces decay rapidly with distance, we know this when we stick a charged balloon to a wall and pull it away ever so slightly and it falls. There is a near-wall zone where the balloon will be attracted to the wall and a "far away" zone where the balloon will drift through the air. This same phenomena happens on a microscopic level and that's sort of what my project looks at: combining a region of ordered motion, repulsion/attraction, with a region of floating diffusion.

The physical picture is a Brownian sphere suspended near a wall. The wall creates an exponentially decaying repulsive potential \(V(x)=V_0 e^{-x/\ell_d}\), while the local diffusivity changes with distance from the wall. This makes the project a nice meeting point between soft-matter diffusion and asymptotically flat potentials.

Introduction: Diffusion and Brownian Motion

Primary source

Per Wikipedia on Brownian Motion: "Scottish botanist Robert Brown, who first described the phenomenon in 1827, while looking through a microscope at pollen ... immersed in water." And wikipedia has the accompanying simulation There is a lot happening in this simulation and the detail can get lost. I didn't build the wikipedia simulator but based on my knowledge and what they're trying to convey with it, here is how I think it works. Note: There is an interplay between what we are trying to communicate to you about Brownian motion and how the simulator works!

  • The noisey/buzzing black dots represent water molecules. They are likely following a particle fluid simulator, just like we had in the Average Value via Fluid Dynamics project. Essentially, we discretize time into chunks \(t_k = k\cdot \Delta t\) and the \(i^{th}\) particle at time \(t_k\) has position and velocity denoted \(p_{i,k},\ v_{i,k}\) respectively. These update using \(F=ma\) and linear approximations: \[p_{i,k} = p_{i,k-1}+v_{i,k-1}\cdot \Delta t\] \[v_{i,k} = v_{i,k-1}+\frac{force(t_{k-1})}{m}\cdot \Delta t \] The force on a given particle is computed via pressure and collision forces and \( m \) is the mass of the water molecule. These are explored more indepth in Average Value via Fluid Dynamics
  • The yellow pollen grain is much larger and its motion is what we call Brownian motion. At any moment in time \(t_k\), the net momentum on the pollen grain is the sum of all the molecular collisions. For programming purposes this just means that at time \(t_k\) we add up all the impacts with the Pollen to get a net velocity vector. We will use capital letters \(P_k,\ V_k\) for the pollen's position and velocity at time \(t_k\), and \(M\) for the mass of the pollen grain. \[P_k = P_{k-1}+ V_{k-1}\cdot \Delta t\] \[V_k = V_{k-1}+\frac{m}{M}\left(\sum_{\text{dot hitting pollen}} v_{i,k}\right)\cdot \Delta t\] If you zoom in on the wikipedia animation you will see an orange \(V_k\) appear at times on the pollen grain.
  • On can imagine the astronomical amount of water molecules hitting a pollen grain and the computational complexity of running an "accurate" simulation. Instead, we can model the trajectory of the pollen grain without the use of buzzing water molecules by thinking of its velocity at time \(t_k\), \(V_k\), as a random vector.

Here is an example of this model. In this example, we fix the step size, \(dx\), and randomly generate \(\theta_k\in [0,2\pi)\) so that \(V_k = dx\cdot (\cos(\theta_k),\ \sin(\theta_k))\). There are two animations but they are generated with the same ranom seed, hence the directions repeat, what changed is the step size - making it easier to see the velocity \(V_k\) at each step.

Small-step walk

A smaller value of \(dx\) makes the walk look more like gentle jitter. The direction still changes randomly at each step, but the visual increments are subtle.

Larger-step walk

Increasing \(dx\) makes the same random orientations much easier to track. This version highlights the individual step vector \(V_k\) and the cumulative position update \(P_k=P_{k-1}+V_k\).

The trajectory traced out on the wikipedia gif is essentially this but with very small step size.

Using Averages

Primary source

A single random walk is interesting, but by itself it does not tell us what the process typically does. In stochastic processes we usually study behavior by running many trials and looking at the ensemble (which is a fancy word for the average of something). If \(X_{k,j}\) is the position of the \(k\)-th trajectory at step \(j\), then each fixed value of \(j\) gives us a whole cloud of outcomes \[ X_{1,j},\ X_{2,j},\ \ldots,\ X_{K,j}. \] The point is that although any one trajectory looks noisy, the collection of many trajectories can reveal stable patterns.

The notebook random_walk_ensemble.ipynb demonstrates this with the same random-walk model used in the Manim animation. First it plots all \(K\) trajectories at once, which makes the overall spread visible. Then it computes the distance of each walk from the origin at each step, \[ \|X_{k,j}\|, \] and shows those values on a scatter plot. This is often more informative than staring at the raw \(x\)- and \(y\)-coordinates separately, because distance from the origin is one clean summary of how far the walk has wandered.

Finally, the notebook averages those distances over all trials and defines \[ s_j=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\|X_{k,j}\|. \] The sequence \(s_j\) is an example of an ensemble statistic: it takes a random object and turns it into something smoother and easier to interpret. This is exactly the philosophy we use later in the project. We do not expect one stochastic trajectory to look tidy, but we can still ask meaningful questions about average position, average distance, spread, and eventually whether the ensemble behaves in a Gaussian way or not.

  • Plot 1 in the notebook shows all trajectories with different colors.
  • Plot 2 shows the full collection of distances \(\|X_{k,j}\|\) against step \(j\).
  • Plot 3 shows the averaged distance \(s_j\), which is much smoother than any one trial.

Boltzmann

Primary source

In statistical mechanics, everything is probabilistic. One of the simplest examples is Brownian motion: even when no obvious force is present, a small particle in a thermal bath jitters unpredictably because of many tiny collisions with the surrounding fluid.

A common way to study this kind of randomness is through an ensemble average. If we simulate many particle paths \(x_t^{(1)}, x_t^{(2)}, \ldots, x_t^{(N)}\) and average them at a fixed time \(t\), we get \(\langle x_t \rangle\), the average position of the whole collection at that time. This kind of averaging appears all over applied mathematics and probability, including diffusion models, the Black-Scholes equation, Langevin dynamics, Monte Carlo methods, and stochastic models in biology and soft matter physics.

In this project the randomness is influenced by a potential field that is strong near a wall, located at \(x=0\), but weak far away, with \(V(x) \to 0\) as \(x \to \infty\). That means there are two competing effects. Close to the wall, the potential strongly shapes the motion; farther away, the force fades and ordinary diffusion becomes more important. The trajectory plots on this page are meant to make that transition visible.

The code builds those trajectories with an overdamped Langevin update: at each time step it computes the local drift coming from the potential, adds a random Brownian increment, and reflects particles away from the wall if they try to cross it. Repeating that step many times gives individual sample paths, and repeating the whole simulation many times gives the ensemble statistics used in the later figures.

Scaling profile

Constant diffusivity

For the exponentially decaying potential, the simulation is compared to the shape of the expected scaling profile. The plotted curve is fit in amplitude at the final time so the visual comparison focuses on shape rather than the exact finite-time prefactor.

Velocity check

Drift validation

The ensemble drift from the simulation tracks \(F(x)/\gamma\), which is the deterministic drift term in the overdamped Langevin model.

Mean-square displacement

The wall-dependent diffusivity models both produce displacement variance that remains close to linear in time, even though the diffusion rate depends on position.

Kurtosis

The displacement distribution is not perfectly Gaussian near the wall. The excess kurtosis stays away from zero for long stretches of time, which is the statistical signature this rebuild is trying to capture.

How to rerun it

The notebook can be opened directly in VS Code and run cell by cell. If you want the script version instead, from the repository root run: 

python3 projects/non-normalizable-boltzmann/non_normalizable_boltzmann.py

Both versions are built around the same model and write the same four PNGs in projects/non-normalizable-boltzmann/images/.

What changed from the old project

This version uses Euler-Maruyama time stepping in Python rather than the original discrete MATLAB walk. That keeps the implementation compact and makes the stochastic equations easier to inspect and modify.
